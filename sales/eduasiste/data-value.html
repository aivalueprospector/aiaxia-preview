Your data, and why more of it makes the Twin smarter

A learning Twin turns a learner's work — assignments, quizzes, reading, the materials a parent or tutor gathers — into a connected picture of how that student is actually doing. But a Twin is only as smart as the data it has seen. This page explains the four kinds of data a Twin learns, what each one lets you ask, and why the more you give it, the more it can tell you about real progress.

The one-line frame: "Your child or your students already produce all of this every week — it just lives scattered across worksheets, apps and your memory. The Twin gathers it into one connected picture, and the more you give it, the more it can show you about how learning is really going."

The four kinds of data

Data comes at different scales. The Twin uses all four together — the big picture of who and what is being learned, the day-to-day work, the threads that tie it together, and the goals you're working toward.

1Macro-data — the entities

The stable map of the learning: the student (or students), the subjects, topics and courses they're working through. It changes slowly and forms the frame everything else hangs on. On its own it answers "who is learning, and what?"

2Event-data — the timeline

Every meaningful thing that happens, time-stamped: an assignment turned in, a quiz scored, a lesson finished, a tricky question asked. Events accumulate fastest and carry the signal of how learning is actually moving. They answer "what did they do, when, and how often?" — and once you have weeks of them, "is this getting better or worse?"

3Micro-data — connections & causes

The threads between students, topics and work — and, crucially, which event caused another. This is where a pile of scores becomes understanding. It answers "how does it all connect, and what's driving the results?" — for example, "the low fractions quiz traces back to the three lessons skipped two weeks earlier."

4Outcome-data — goals & progress

The learning goals you actually care about, with a number attached and tracked over time. As work flows in, progress updates on its own. This answers the question a parent or mentor cares about most: "are they moving forward, and by how much?"

What more data unlocks

Each kind of data is useful alone. Together — and at volume — they compound. More data doesn't just mean more storage; it means the Twin can answer questions it simply couldn't before.

Give the Twin more……and you unlock
More students + workA fuller picture. Isolated scores become a connected view of the learner. The Twin can surface where attention is going — and the topics that come up again and again.
More coverageVisible gaps. With more in, the Twin can point out what's missing or stale — a topic never practiced, a subject gone quiet for weeks.
More work over timeCause and effect. Enough history lets the Twin link causes to outcomes — "missed lessons → low quiz" — answering in seconds what used to mean digging through a folder of worksheets.
More historySharper goals. A richer record makes progress tracking accurate and automatic, instead of a chart someone updates by hand.

10 pieces of work vs. 50. With ten, the Twin answers questions about those ten. With fifty, it starts connecting them: a quiz score links to the topic it covered, the lessons that led up to it, and the pattern across the term — and you can ask "which students stalled after week 3, and on what topic?" and get an answer from real work, not a guess. That gap only widens the more you feed it.

What every Twin includes

  • Private and encrypted. A learner's data is theirs — isolated, and never used to train anyone else's model.
  • Yours to take. Export anytime — the data is portable, not locked in.
  • No lock-in. Cancel anytime.

The takeaway for a family or a school is simple: the Twin gets more useful the more of the learning it sees, and everything you put in stays private and stays yours.
